# 3. The Bohemian Digital Philosophy

## 3.1 What “bohemian” means here

“Bohemian” does not mean decorative whimsy, tie-dye copy, excessive wordplay, or pretending the company operates from an enchanted forest.

It describes the operating philosophy:

- independent thinking instead of inherited agency dogma;
- creative adaptation instead of one-size-fits-all packages;
- craftsmanship supported by technical systems;
- research before certainty;
- direct relationships instead of layers of account management;
- resourcefulness instead of expensive bloat;
- curiosity about how each market actually works;
- willingness to combine design, code, SEO, research, and business logic when the problem crosses disciplines;
- respect for client ownership and freedom to leave.

Boho should feel creative because it makes intelligent, market-specific decisions, not because every heading contains a bee pun.

## 3.2 The core belief

Every industry is different. Every local market is different. Every company earns money differently.

A contractor selling a $20,000 project does not need the same website as a café. An ecommerce store does not need the same SEO plan as a clinic. A professional-services firm does not earn trust through the same signals as a plumber.

Boho meets the business where it is.

That means learning:

- what the business sells;
- which services or products are most valuable;
- what a qualified lead looks like;
- where customers search;
- which objections stop customers from acting;
- which signals make the business look trustworthy;
- where competitors are strong or weak;
- what the current website and provider setup can realistically support;
- which improvements are worth their cost.

## 3.3 Small by design

Boho should not apologize for being a small shop.

The positioning is:

> Small by design. Broad enough to solve the real problem. Lean enough to keep the work close to the people doing it.

Large agencies can be excellent, but their operating models often include sales overhead, account layers, standardized packages, and reporting systems designed for larger clients. Boho is built for businesses that need direct thinking, practical execution, and fewer layers between the problem and the person fixing it.

Lower prices are not framed as a discount.

They are framed as an efficiency advantage:

> Better work per dollar because the company is designed intelligently.

## 3.4 Creativity with discipline

Creativity is not decoration. It is the ability to find a better route through a specific constraint.

Boho should be willing to:

- redesign the website when SEO alone cannot fix weak positioning;
- repair the existing site when a rebuild would be wasteful;
- migrate a business away from a provider when ownership is the real problem;
- prioritize one high-value service page over twenty generic articles;
- build a local market report before recommending a large campaign;
- use automation to reduce repetitive labor while keeping human judgment in charge;
- create a custom reporting method when standard dashboards hide the decision;
- recommend no ongoing retainer when a focused project is enough.

## 3.5 Ownership as a design principle

A client should be more capable after working with Boho, not more dependent.

Whenever possible:

- accounts should be owned by the client;
- domains should be registered to the client;
- analytics and search accounts should be accessible to the client;
- changes should be documented;
- migrations should leave a readable trail;
- content and website assets should be portable;
- ongoing work should continue because it is useful, not because leaving is painful.

## 3.6 Evidence without false certainty

Boho should be technical and research-minded without pretending digital marketing is laboratory physics.

Search, visibility, trust, and leads are affected by many variables. Correlation is useful for forming priorities, but correlation is not automatically causation.

The public philosophy is:

> Signals are clues. We use them to form a testable theory of what may improve the business, then compare changes against actual customer actions.

The causal chain Boho usually investigates is:

**Technical access → relevant visibility → customer understanding → trust → action → business value**

Not every project reaches every stage. The purpose of the work is to identify where the chain is weak.

## 3.7 The optimization model

Use the following model internally and explain it in simple language publicly.

For a possible improvement \(i\), define a directional priority score:

\[
P_i = \frac{V_i \times I_i \times C_i \times M_i}{E_i + R_i}
\]

Where:

- \(V_i\) = business value of the affected customer action;
- \(I_i\) = estimated size of the possible improvement;
- \(C_i\) = confidence in the relationship between the change and the desired outcome;
- \(M_i\) = measurability of the result;
- \(E_i\) = implementation effort and cost;
- \(R_i\) = technical, operational, legal, or reputational risk.

This is not presented as precise mathematics. It is a disciplined way to avoid optimizing whatever happens to be easiest to graph.

Public-facing explanation:

> We rank opportunities by business value, likely impact, confidence, measurability, cost, and risk. Then we start with the strongest case.

## 3.8 Claims Boho refuses to make

Boho does not claim:

- guaranteed rankings;
- guaranteed lead volume;
- guaranteed revenue;
- secret access to search engines;
- instant SEO results;
- universal ranking formulas;
- magic AI-search placement;
- that every traffic increase is valuable;
- that every business needs a monthly retainer;
- that publishing more content is always better;
- that a redesign automatically improves performance;
- that one metric proves causation;
- that complex reporting is automatically intelligent.

# 7. Global Visual Design System

## 7.1 Design concept

Use **controlled eclecticism**.

The page may shift visual mood as the visitor scrolls, but every section must feel like part of the same company. Variety comes from composition, texture, and emphasis. Coherence comes from typography, spacing, buttons, grid, palette, and repeating motifs.

The visual identity should communicate:

- creative judgment;
- technical precision;
- independent craft;
- research and evidence;
- premium taste without premium-agency posturing.

## 7.2 Visual voices

### Voice A: Cinematic

Use for:

- homepage hero;
- final calls to action;
- selected service heroes;
- emergency page.

Characteristics:

- dark obsidian or graphite background;
- large expressive typography;
- restrained mosaic glow;
- asymmetrical composition;
- one dominant visual object;
- minimal copy.

### Voice B: Editorial

Use for:

- philosophy;
- problem framing;
- About;
- Industries;
- Learn.

Characteristics:

- warm ivory or parchment backgrounds;
- strong reading widths;
- pull quotes;
- margin notes;
- layered paper or collage blocks;
- human pacing.

### Voice C: Research

Use for:

- method;
- Lab;
- audits;
- market research;
- technical SEO;
- proof.

Characteristics:

- grids;
- map paths;
- data labels;
- report fragments;
- nodes;
- subtle heat-map fields;
- measured technical annotation.

### Voice D: Mosaic Workshop

Use for:

- service gallery;
- website-design showcase;
- selected cards;
- portfolio concepts.

Characteristics:

- irregular but disciplined card compositions;
- stained-glass or ceramic-mosaic color blocks;
- layered panels;
- tactile texture;
- distinct module shapes held within one grid.

Do not introduce a fifth major visual language without replacing one of these.

## 7.3 Color palette

Use this palette as the starting system. Adjust only when required for accessibility.

| Token | Hex | Use |
|---|---:|---|
| Ink | `#111214` | Primary dark background and text |
| Graphite | `#1D2024` | Secondary dark surfaces |
| Warm Ivory | `#F4F0E7` | Primary light background |
| Parchment | `#E5DDCF` | Editorial panels |
| Burnished Gold | `#C69745` | Primary accent and selected CTA details |
| Verdigris | `#2D6C68` | Research and map accents |
| Copper | `#A75B3D` | Warm accent and emergency detail |
| Dusty Plum | `#6A405F` | Mosaic depth |
| Blue Glass | `#4F7487` | Iridescent mosaic accent |
| Sage Gray | `#AAB3A3` | Quiet supporting accent |
| White | `#FFFFFF` | High-contrast text where needed |

Rules:

1. Do not use pure black as the default; use Ink.
2. Do not pair black and bright yellow in a way that resembles hazard tape.
3. Burnished Gold should occupy limited visual area.
4. Use Verdigris, Copper, Plum, and Blue Glass as a mosaic family, not as unrelated rainbow colors.
5. Every text/background pairing must pass accessible contrast.
6. Use no more than two accent colors prominently in one section.

## 7.4 Typography

Preferred open web-font direction:

- **Display:** Newsreader, Fraunces, or another expressive editorial serif with variable weights.
- **Body/UI:** Manrope, Inter, or another highly readable modern sans serif.

Recommended pairing:

- Headlines: **Newsreader**
- Body, labels, buttons, navigation: **Manrope**

Fallback:

```css
display: "Newsreader", Georgia, serif;
body: "Manrope", Inter, Arial, sans-serif;
```

Rules:

1. Use the serif for large headlines, selected pull quotes, and editorial emphasis.
2. Use the sans serif for body copy, navigation, buttons, data labels, forms, and technical annotations.
3. Avoid all-caps body text.
4. Use uppercase only for short eyebrows or labels.
5. Keep body text between roughly 17px and 20px on desktop, with comfortable line height.
6. Keep reading widths near 60–75 characters.
7. Avoid ultra-thin font weights.

## 7.5 Grid and spacing

Desktop:

- maximum content width: approximately 1280–1360px;
- standard reading column: 680–780px;
- 12-column grid;
- generous outer margins;
- section padding: approximately 96–144px depending on intensity.

Tablet:

- 8-column grid;
- section padding: approximately 72–96px.

Mobile:

- 4-column grid;
- side padding: 20–24px;
- section padding: approximately 56–80px;
- no horizontal scroll;
- no tiny mosaic fragments containing essential text.

## 7.6 Shape language

Use:

- mostly square or lightly rounded panels;
- occasional irregular mosaic edge or clipped corner;
- modest corner radius, not oversized bubble cards;
- thin rules and annotated lines;
- hexagonal geometry only as a subtle underlying grid.

Avoid:

- every card having a different shape;
- giant pill-shaped containers;
- repeated honeycomb icons;
- beveled fake 3D panels;
- excessive glassmorphism.

## 7.7 Bee and mosaic motif

The bee should function as a structural metaphor, not a mascot.

Acceptable expressions:

- abstract wing geometry;
- cellular mosaic patterns;
- pollen-like signal trails;
- small hex-grid fragments;
- an iridescent wing texture;
- modular systems that suggest a hive;
- motion paths that suggest gathering and connecting signals.

Avoid:

- cartoon bees;
- honey drips;
- bee puns in every section;
- literal beehives as section backgrounds;
- repeated black/yellow striping.

## 7.8 Motion

Motion should be quiet and optional.

Allowed:

- slow mosaic shimmer;
- subtle parallax on decorative layers;
- line-path drawing in diagrams;
- small card lift or border change on hover;
- gentle section reveal;
- animated counters only when values are real.

Requirements:

- honor `prefers-reduced-motion`;
- no auto-playing background video;
- no scroll-jacking;
- no essential content hidden behind animation;
- no constant floating or pulsing elements;
- animations should not delay interaction.

## 7.9 Buttons and links

Primary button:

- solid Burnished Gold or Warm Ivory depending on background;
- Ink text;
- clear rectangular shape with modest radius;
- medium-large target size.

Secondary button:

- transparent or low-fill;
- strong visible border;
- high-contrast text.

Text link:

- underlined on hover and focus;
- optional arrow that moves slightly on hover;
- never rely on color alone.

Button labels should be specific:

- Get a Visibility Check
- Plan a Website
- Plan the Rescue
- Improve Local Visibility
- Ask About Ongoing Support
- View the Lab

Avoid:

- Learn More repeated everywhere;
- Get Started without context;
- Submit for important forms.

## 7.10 Navigation

Use a sticky but quiet header.

Desktop:

- logo left;
- navigation centered or right;
- primary CTA on far right;
- transparent over hero, then solid or blurred Ink/Ivory surface after scroll.

Mobile:

- logo;
- primary CTA or compact CTA icon/text;
- menu trigger;
- full-height menu with large readable links;
- Contact and Emergency Help visible.

The header must not change its entire design between pages.

## 7.11 Footer

Footer content:

- Boho Digital Services logo/name;
- short brand statement;
- Services links;
- Industries links;
- Lab and Learn links;
- Pricing, About, Contact;
- Privacy, Terms, Accessibility;
- email address when confirmed;
- social links only when real and active;
- copyright using current year dynamically;
- small line: “Websites, search visibility, migration, and growth systems built from research.”

Do not place fake locations, phone numbers, memberships, or certifications in the footer.

# 32. Content and SEO Engineering

## 32.1 Core SEO rules

1. Give every published page a unique title and meta description.
2. Use one clear H1 per page.
3. Maintain logical heading order.
4. Use canonical URLs.
5. Generate an XML sitemap for published, indexable pages.
6. Exclude draft, thin, test, thank-you, and private pages from indexing.
7. Create a human-readable redirect map before replacing the old site.
8. Use descriptive internal links.
9. Do not stuff location or service keywords.
10. Do not generate fake location pages.
11. Do not publish thin glossary or industry pages.
12. Use image alt text that describes the image's purpose, not a keyword pile.
13. Set Open Graph and social-sharing fields.
14. Provide a useful favicon and social-share image.
15. Preserve meaningful old URLs or redirect them to the closest relevant page.

# 34. Accessibility, Performance, and Security

## 34.1 Accessibility target

Build toward WCAG 2.2 AA principles and test the actual output rather than assuming the builder handled it.

Required checks:

- meaningful heading hierarchy;
- keyboard navigation;
- visible focus states;
- sufficient color contrast;
- readable text sizing;
- form labels and errors;
- alt text;
- landmark regions;
- skip-to-content link;
- no essential hover-only content;
- reduced-motion support;
- accessible mobile menu;
- no text embedded in images when normal text can be used;
- descriptive link labels;
- adequate touch targets;
- correct language attribute;
- sensible reading order.

## 34.2 Performance target

The visual richness must not become a punishment for mobile visitors.

Requirements:

1. Avoid autoplay video.
2. Prefer optimized AVIF/WebP images and SVG/CSS geometry.
3. Lazy-load below-the-fold media.
4. Reserve media dimensions to reduce layout shift.
5. Limit font families and weights.
6. Preload only critical fonts and hero media.
7. Minimize third-party scripts.
8. Avoid excessive animation libraries.
9. Do not ship full-resolution concept mockups when smaller responsive images suffice.
10. Test on a midrange mobile device and throttled connection.
11. Ensure forms and navigation work before decorative scripts load.
12. Keep the hero useful if the abstract visual fails.

Performance goals should be treated as validation targets, not guaranteed scores independent of the chosen builder.

## 34.3 Security and privacy

1. Use HTTPS.
2. Do not expose API keys or secrets in client code.
3. Use platform-native secret management.
4. Use least-privilege access.
5. Do not collect passwords through forms.
6. Sanitize and validate form input.
7. Add spam protection with an accessible fallback.
8. Keep dependencies and integrations current where the builder exposes them.
9. Do not embed untrusted scripts.
10. Verify all external links.
11. Document analytics, form processors, embeds, and cookies.
12. Use authorized platform invitations for client access.
13. Do not publish staging URLs containing private client data.
14. Prepare a backup/export path when supported.

## 34.4 Legal and claim review

Before production publication, review:

- price ranges;
- service availability;
- response-time language;
- business identity;
- email and contact details;
- privacy policy;
- terms;
- regulated-industry language;
- claims about ownership and portability;
- examples and in-house brand descriptions;
- third-party trademarks;
- testimonials or client results added later.

# 40. Acceptance Tests

The site is not ready merely because every route renders.

## 40.1 Brand test

Pass when:

- the site feels creative and technically credible;
- the bee appears as an abstract system motif, not a mascot costume;
- the palette is mosaic-like rather than black/yellow;
- visual variation exists without navigation confusion;
- the Bohemian Digital philosophy is clear;
- website design, provider rescue, SEO, lead generation, research, and ongoing support are all unmistakable.

Fail when:

- the site resembles a generic agency template;
- every section is a card grid;
- every section uses the same background;
- the design depends on corny forest, owl, hive, or honey language;
- visual experimentation overwhelms readability;
- the homepage feels like six unrelated sites.

## 40.2 Content test

Pass when:

- the homepage presents one idea per section;
- body copy is readable and specific;
- no fake proof appears;
- price ranges are labeled as planning ranges;
- migration boundaries are lawful and clear;
- ongoing support is not sold as unlimited work;
- every buyer bucket has a meaningful page;
- technical terms are explained or linked;
- copy does not collapse into generic “growth partner” language.

Fail when:

- pages are mostly slogans;
- services are described only through buzzwords;
- industry pages are keyword-swapped duplicates;
- the homepage contains giant bullet walls;
- the builder rewrites approved copy into corporate filler;
- empty research pages are published.

## 40.3 Navigation test

Pass when:

- a visitor can reach Services, Industries, Lab, Learn, Pricing, About, Contact, Start, and Emergency;
- the primary CTA is obvious;
- breadcrumbs appear on deep pages;
- mobile navigation is keyboard and screen-reader usable;
- related-page modules provide sensible next steps;
- no page traps the visitor.

## 40.4 Conversion test

Pass when:

- visibility-check, contact, and emergency forms are distinct;
- form labels and errors are accessible;
- no form requests passwords or sensitive data;
- confirmation states work;
- CTA events are prepared;
- email/processor activation is approved;
- phone and booking links work when present;
- the visitor understands what happens after submission.

## 40.5 Responsive test

Test:

- 320px mobile;
- common modern mobile widths;
- tablet portrait and landscape;
- laptop;
- large desktop;
- browser zoom at 200%.

Pass when:

- no horizontal overflow;
- no clipped headings;
- no unreadable mosaic text;
- no hidden CTA;
- no broken card order;
- diagrams become understandable stacked layouts;
- forms remain usable;
- navigation remains clear.

## 40.6 Accessibility test

Pass when:

- all content is keyboard accessible;
- focus is visible;
- contrast is sufficient;
- headings are logical;
- images have appropriate alt text;
- decorative images are ignored by assistive technology;
- forms have labels and meaningful errors;
- motion can be reduced;
- link purpose is understandable;
- the mobile menu is accessible;
- the reading order matches the visual order.

## 40.7 SEO and migration test

Pass when:

- every indexable page has unique metadata;
- canonical URLs are correct;
- sitemap contains only publishable pages;
- drafts and thank-you pages are noindexed;
- old URLs have an explicit disposition;
- redirects point to relevant destinations;
- no fake location pages exist;
- structured data reflects visible truth;
- social-share previews work;
- robots rules do not accidentally block the site.

## 40.8 Performance test

Pass when:

- the site remains useful before decorative media loads;
- no autoplay background video exists;
- images are responsive and optimized;
- fonts are limited;
- third-party scripts are restrained;
- motion is light;
- forms and navigation work under slow conditions;
- no visual effect causes major layout instability.

## 40.9 Truth test

Before approval, search the site for:

- best;
- leading;
- expert;
- proven;
- guaranteed;
- award;
- client;
- customer;
- revenue;
- traffic;
- ranking;
- certified;
- partner;
- years;
- team;
- location names.

Every occurrence must be supported, qualified, or removed.

# 41. Stop Conditions for the Website Builder

Stop and leave a visible note instead of guessing when:

1. A real address, phone number, email, founder name, legal entity, or social link is required but not supplied.
2. A price or scope conflicts with a later approved source.
3. A form requires a processor, API key, or email destination that has not been approved.
4. The builder wants to publish or connect the production domain.
5. A page requires a real case study, testimonial, metric, or screenshot that does not exist.
6. A third-party integration requires payment or account modification.
7. Existing URL inventory is unavailable for redirect planning.
8. A regulated-industry claim requires professional or legal review.
9. A design effect materially harms readability, accessibility, or performance.
10. The builder cannot create the requested visual system without substituting a generic template.

For each blocker, report:

- what is missing;
- what was attempted;
- the smallest human decision required;
- the safest fallback.

# 42. Definition of Done

The draft is ready for human review when:

1. Every launch route exists in draft.
2. The sitemap and navigation match this document.
3. The homepage follows the eleven-section narrative.
4. The four primary capabilities are obvious:
   - website design;
   - migration/provider rescue;
   - SEO/visibility/lead generation;
   - ongoing support.
5. The five buyer buckets are present and distinct.
6. The Bohemian Digital philosophy appears on Home and About without becoming decorative wordplay.
7. Visual voices vary by section but use one design system.
8. All supplied body copy is present or intentionally adapted for layout without changing the claim.
9. No fake proof, metrics, clients, testimonials, or locations appear.
10. Forms are designed but external sending remains unactivated until approved.
11. Draft legal pages reflect the actual planned data flow and remain marked for review.
12. Accessibility, responsive, performance, SEO, and truth checks are complete.
13. Redirect planning is documented but not deployed.
14. The production domain remains untouched.
15. A concise handoff note lists:
    - built pages;
    - unfinished pages;
    - integrations awaiting approval;
    - missing business details;
    - accessibility issues;
    - performance issues;
    - redirect dependencies;
    - recommended next actions.

# 43. Final Controlling Statement

When design, copy, SEO convention, and builder defaults conflict, use this priority order:

1. Truth
2. Clarity
3. Customer action
4. Accessibility
5. Ownership and safety
6. Research and evidence
7. Visual distinction
8. Search optimization
9. Builder convenience

The final website should make this promise visible without having to announce it repeatedly:

> Boho gives businesses more useful digital work per dollar by studying the market, designing or repairing the right systems, preserving ownership, and improving the signals most likely to create trust and customer action.
